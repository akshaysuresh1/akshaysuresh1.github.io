Fast Radio Bursts from M87

Fast radio bursts (FRBs) are bright, millisecond-duration coherent radio pulses of extragalactic origin. Though over 600 FRB sources have been discovered, the physical engines powering FRB emission are still unknown.

Precise burst localization and host galaxy characterization are essential to decipher identities of FRB progenitors. An FRB survey of local galaxy clusters, therefore, bears great scientific promise. Burst discoveries in such a survey will enable sensitive multi-wavelength follow-up to constrain potential FRB source models.

The red elliptical galaxy M87 has been a target of intense debate in historical radio transient surveys, owing to the heavily scrutinized detection of dispersed bursts by Linscott & Erkes (1980). Leveraging modern advancements in instrumentation and computing capacity, we observed M87 for 10 hours with the Arecibo radio telescope in 2019.

We found no evidence of dispersed pulses in our 1.15–1.75 GHz data above a 7σ threshold of 1.4 Jy ms. Thus, we constrain the burst rate from M87 to ≲ 0.1 bursts/hour, the most stringent upper limit derived to date. We postulate a non-astrophysical origin for the Linscott & Erkes (1980) pulses based on their non-detection in several subsequent surveys of M87 with increased sensitivity.
